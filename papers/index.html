AI as Modern Alchemy

RESEARCH PAPER SERIES · FOUR PAPERS · EPISTEMOLOGY · EMERGENCE · ECONOMICS · SYNTHESIS
Four papers mapping the transmutation of computation into cognition. Each examines a different facet — epistemology, emergence, economics, synthesis — of the same fundamental problem: we are building intelligence we cannot understand, at costs we are only beginning to measure.
PAPER SERIES
Research Program

I. The Stone's Shadow: Optimization Without Understanding

PAPER I WORKING PAPER · v0.1 · AI Epistemology · ~4,200 words
The growing divergence between optimization performance and mechanistic understanding in neural systems. Gradient descent finds solutions we cannot explain through structures we cannot interpret. Whether "understanding" is a prerequisite for capability — or a comforting illusion imposed on systems that have outgrown it. Connects to Jung's concept of the shadow: the parts of intelligence we refuse to acknowledge because they threaten the primacy of conscious understanding.
Keywords: mechanistic interpretability, optimization, epistemology, Jungian shadow, neural network opacity, gradient descent, loss landscapes
Empirical foundation: CHIMERA — 847K traces showing confidence-correctness disconnect (I(C;Y) = 0.23 bits)

II. Digital Philosopher's Stone: Phase Transitions in Capability

PAPER II WORKING PAPER · v0.1 · Emergence Theory · ~3,800 words
Capability emergence analyzed as phase transition phenomenon. Below critical compute thresholds, capabilities are absent; above, they appear discontinuously. Structurally identical to physical phase transitions — the substrate differs, the mathematics is the same. Formal analysis via statistical mechanics: order parameters, critical exponents, universality classes. Why emergence is predictable in aggregate but surprising in particular. Implications for predictability and governance of frontier systems.
Keywords: emergence, phase transitions, scaling laws, critical phenomena, statistical mechanics, capability jumps, governance
Empirical foundation: alignment-tax-quantifier — scaling behavior across 125M–70B parameters

III. Economic Transmutation: Why This Time Is Different

PAPER III WORKING PAPER · v0.1 · AI Economics · ~5,100 words
Every previous technological disruption displaced specific skill-sets while creating demand for others. AI displaces the meta-skill: the ability to learn and adapt. Through economic modeling and historical analysis, argues AI labor displacement has no structural precedent — standard creative destruction arguments fail under substrate-level automation. Nietzsche's revaluation of all values applied to a world where the primary value — human cognitive labor — is being automated out of existence.
Keywords: labor economics, creative destruction, automation, Nietzsche, revaluation, cognitive labor, technological unemployment, substrate-level displacement
Empirical foundation: alignment-tax-quantifier — τ(N) = aN⁻ᵇ + c quantifies the cost of safety constraints

IV. Lead Into Gold: A Unified Theory of Artificial Transmutation

PAPER IV CAPSTONE · 2026 · Research Synthesis
The synthesis. Conformal prediction as quantification of epistemic limits. Alignment tax as cost of control. Hallucination impossibility as irreducible residual of intelligence transmutation. Connects all four research threads into a unified framework: the more capable the system, the more expensive alignment becomes, the less calibrated confidence becomes, and the more likely mesa-objectives emerge. Three functions of compute scale — τ(N), H(N,T), ΔL(N) — revealed as projections of the same underlying process. This is not pessimism. It is measurement.
Keywords: conformal prediction, alignment tax, hallucination impossibility, information theory, scaling laws, power-law analysis, alchemy, transmutation, unified framework
Integrates all implementations: conformal-multimodal · alignment-tax-quantifier · CHIMERA · SHOGGOTH

READING ORDER
How to Read This Series

The papers can be read independently but are designed as a sequence. Paper I establishes the epistemological problem: we optimize systems we cannot understand. Paper II explains why this understanding gap widens discontinuously at scale. Paper III traces the economic consequences. Paper IV unifies the analysis through formal mathematical connections between the implementations.

Each paper references specific results from the software implementations. The code is not supplementary material — it is the empirical foundation. Reading the papers without running the code is reading the theory without the experiment.
